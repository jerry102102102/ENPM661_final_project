# Relation to Prior Work and Our Extensions

## 1. Purpose of This Note

This note is meant to complement the existing project documents.

The current project materials already cover:
- the core algorithm and mathematical derivation,
- the implementation details of the sampled nonholonomic temporal roadmap,
- the ACTEA method definition,
- and the evaluation story.

What is still useful for the final paper is a focused explanation of:

This document is written for that purpose.

---

## 2. Upstream Research Line

The main upstream research line for this project comes from **temporal roadmap planning in dynamic environments**.

The most important papers in that line, for our project, are:

- **T-PRM: Temporal Probabilistic Roadmap for Path Planning in Dynamic Environments**
- **TA-PRM\***: Time-Aware Probabilistic Roadmaps for Multi-Query Path Planning in Dynamic Environments

These papers are attractive because they preserve the roadmap idea of PRM while adding explicit reasoning about **time-dependent safety** in dynamic scenes.

In simple terms, the upstream literature starts from this idea:

```text
A roadmap node or edge is not only spatially valid or invalid.
Its validity also depends on when the robot reaches it.
```

That idea is the main conceptual bridge into our project.

---

## 3. What We Inherited from Prior Work

Our project directly inherits several major ideas from the prior temporal-roadmap literature.

### 3.1 Reusable roadmap structure

Like PRM, T-PRM and TA-PRM* preserve the multi-query advantage of a roadmap:

```text
Build the roadmap once.
Reuse it for many start-goal queries.
```

This multi-query viewpoint is central to our project as well.  
Our method is not a one-shot replanning trick; it is a reusable temporal-roadmap framework.

### 3.2 Time-dependent validity

The upstream papers emphasize that static geometric validity is not enough in dynamic environments.

A node or edge may be:
- safe at one time,
- unsafe at another,
- safe again later.

That motivates storing temporal knowledge on top of the roadmap rather than performing purely static planning.

### 3.3 Time-aware graph search

The upstream papers move from ordinary graph search to time-aware search.

That means the search state must account not only for where the robot is in the roadmap, but also when it arrives there.

This idea is preserved in our project through temporal search labels of the form:

```text
(node_id, arrival_time)
```

### 3.4 Multi-query dynamic-environment reasoning

The upstream temporal-roadmap idea is especially attractive because it tries to preserve reusable planning structure even in dynamic environments.

That exact motivation remains central in our project:
we want to answer many temporal queries on the same roadmap without repeating the full cost of online spatiotemporal reasoning every time.

---

## 4. Why the Upstream Formulation Does Not Directly Fit Our Target Problem

Even though the prior papers give us the right conceptual starting point, their original assumptions do not directly match the robot and planner setting of our project.

### 4.1 Holonomic / point-robot assumptions

The T-PRM paper is formulated for holonomic robots with first-order dynamics and, for simplicity, treats the robot as a point with no physical extension.

That is a clean research formulation, but it does not match our main target setting:
a robot whose motion is generated through **nonholonomic motion primitives** and whose state includes heading.

Our project therefore cannot directly use the original formulation without modification.

### 4.2 Node-centric temporal validity is too weak for our setting

The T-PRM-style formulation is most naturally expressed in terms of **time availability at nodes**.

That is reasonable when:
- the robot is holonomic or simplified,
- and node arrival-time safety is the dominant concern.

But for our project, the roadmap edges are not simple straight-line connectors between point states.
They are **sampled nonholonomic primitive trajectories**.
Therefore, safety must be reasoned about along the edge trajectory itself, not only at nodes.

### 4.3 Nonholonomic reachability matters

In our project, a state is not just `(x, y)`.  
It is `(x, y, theta)`.

This changes the roadmap problem substantially:
- not every nearby node is directly reachable,
- heading compatibility matters,
- and local connections must be generated by motion primitives rather than by a holonomic straight-line local planner.

So although the upstream papers motivate time-aware roadmaps, they do not directly solve our nonholonomic edge-construction problem.

---

## 5. Our Main Extensions over Prior Work

Our project can be understood as a structured extension of the temporal-roadmap idea to a nonholonomic roadmap setting.

## Extension 1 — Sampled nonholonomic roadmap instead of holonomic point-node roadmap

The first major extension is at the roadmap level.

Instead of using roadmap nodes that are only spatial waypoints, our roadmap uses:

```text
node = (x, y, theta)
```

and instead of simple holonomic local connectors, we construct edges using:

```text
statically valid nonholonomic motion-primitive rollouts
```

This is a major shift from the upstream formulation.

What this changes:
- roadmap connectivity depends on heading and primitive feasibility,
- the roadmap becomes compatible with nonholonomic motion,
- and temporal reasoning must now be attached to full primitive trajectories.

---

## Extension 2 — Temporal search over sampled nonholonomic edges

The second extension is at query time.

The upstream literature motivates time-aware graph search.
We retain that idea, but apply it to a nonholonomic sampled roadmap.

Our temporal search labels are:

```text
(node_id, arrival_time)
```

The planner:
1. clones the reusable sampled roadmap for each query,
2. inserts temporary start/goal nodes,
3. connects them using the same primitive-based local connection logic,
4. and runs temporal A* over the roadmap.

So the project keeps the upstream time-aware search idea, but grounds it in a nonholonomic primitive-roadmap structure.

---

## Extension 3 — Edge-level temporal reasoning instead of only node-level time availability

This is one of the most important conceptual changes.

In the T-PRM paper notes, node availability is the natural representation:
the planner asks whether a roadmap node is safe at the arrival time.

In our project, that is no longer sufficient because a nonholonomic primitive edge may be dynamically unsafe even if both endpoint nodes appear safe.

Therefore, our project moves temporal validity from:
- primarily node-centered availability

to:
- **edge-centered temporal validity**

This is a meaningful methodological extension, not just an implementation tweak.

---

## Extension 4 — ACTEA: Analytic Continuous-Time Edge Annotation

This is the main new method contribution introduced by our project.

ACTEA stands for:

```text
Analytic Continuous-Time Edge Annotation
```

For each roadmap edge and dynamic-obstacle scenario, ACTEA computes:
- blocked departure-time intervals,
- valid departure-time intervals,
- and edge-level temporal metadata that can be reused across queries.

This is different from simply checking dynamic collisions online during search.
It is also different from a sampled bin-cache approximation.

The key contribution of ACTEA is that it turns repeated online edge-time validity checking into reusable continuous-time interval metadata on roadmap edges.

Under the implemented piecewise-linear primitive trajectory model, ACTEA is exact with respect to online temporal validation.

This is the clearest point at which our project goes beyond merely adapting T-PRM at the engineering level.

---

## Extension 5 — A clearer separation between framework and annotation layer

Another useful way to describe our contribution is to separate:

### Framework contribution
```text
Sampled Nonholonomic Temporal Roadmap
```

This is the planner structure:
- sampled nonholonomic roadmap,
- primitive-based edge generation,
- temporal query over `(node_id, arrival_time)`.

### Method contribution
```text
ACTEA
```

This is the temporal annotation layer:
- continuous-time blocked/valid edge intervals,
- reusable edge-level temporal metadata,
- continuous-time temporal lookup before online fallback.

This separation is important in the final paper because the experiments support these two contributions differently:
- the hard-scene benchmark mainly validates the framework,
- the correctness and repeated-query experiments mainly validate ACTEA.

## 6. What We Did Not Simply Copy

It is useful to state explicitly what our project does **not** do.

### 6.1 We did not simply reproduce node time-availability T-PRM
The upstream T-PRM idea uses time-aware roadmap reasoning, but our project does not stop at node availability.
We move to edge-level temporal annotation, which is more appropriate for sampled nonholonomic primitive edges.

### 6.2 We did not stay in the holonomic point-robot regime
The original literature uses assumptions that make the temporal-roadmap formulation cleaner, but less directly applicable to our target robot setting.
Our project explicitly addresses the nonholonomic roadmap version of the problem.

### 6.3 We did not make ACTEA just a cache trick
ACTEA is not presented merely as a runtime cache.
It is framed as a continuous-time temporal annotation method with explicit edge-time semantics and correctness validation under the implemented trajectory model.

## 7. Clean Summary of the Research Relationship

A concise way to describe the relationship to prior work is:

```text
Prior temporal-roadmap methods such as T-PRM and TA-PRM* introduced the key
idea that roadmap validity in dynamic environments depends on time, not only
space. Our project inherits that multi-query temporal-roadmap viewpoint, but
extends it to sampled nonholonomic primitive roadmaps whose states include
heading and whose edges are full motion-primitive trajectories. Because
nonholonomic primitive edges require temporal reasoning along the edge itself,
we move from node-centric time availability toward edge-centric temporal
annotation. Our main added method contribution is ACTEA, which computes
continuous-time blocked and valid departure intervals for roadmap edges under
the implemented piecewise-linear primitive trajectory model.
```

This paragraph can almost be used directly in the final paper.

## 9. Suggested Final Wording

### Short paragraph version

We draw inspiration from prior temporal-roadmap methods, especially T-PRM and TA-PRM*, which extend probabilistic roadmaps with time-dependent safety reasoning for dynamic environments. However, those formulations are most natural for holonomic or simplified point-robot settings. Our project adapts the temporal-roadmap idea to a sampled nonholonomic roadmap whose states include heading and whose edges are motion-primitive trajectories. This shift makes edge-level temporal reasoning essential. To support that setting, we introduce ACTEA, an analytic continuous-time edge annotation layer that computes blocked and valid departure-time intervals for roadmap edges under the implemented piecewise-linear primitive model.

### Bullet version

- **Inherited idea from prior work:** reusable temporal roadmap reasoning in dynamic environments  
- **Mismatch with prior assumptions:** upstream formulations are cleaner for holonomic / point-robot settings  
- **Our roadmap-level extension:** sampled nonholonomic roadmap with `(x, y, theta)` nodes and primitive edges  
- **Our query-level extension:** temporal search over `(node_id, arrival_time)`  
- **Our main method contribution:** ACTEA, a continuous-time edge annotation layer for blocked/valid departure intervals

## 10. Honest Boundary of the Contribution

To keep the final report credible, we should describe the contribution boundary honestly.

Strong and fair claim:

> We adapt the temporal-roadmap idea from prior work to a sampled nonholonomic primitive-roadmap setting and introduce ACTEA as an edge-level continuous-time temporal annotation method.

Claim to avoid:

> We fully generalize T-PRM / TA-PRM* to all nonholonomic systems with exact continuous dynamics.

That would be too strong.

The more honest statement is:
- exactness is with respect to the implemented piecewise-linear primitive model,
- and the main contribution is a clean nonholonomic roadmap adaptation plus ACTEA.
